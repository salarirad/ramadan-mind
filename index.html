Key findings

What the research revealed

Across multiple studies and methods, fasting during Ramadan produces measurable and consistent effects on how people think, decide, and interact.

↓
Risk-taking
People become more risk-averse during Ramadan, preferring smaller certain gains over larger uncertain rewards — a shift that dissipates after the month ends.
↑
Cooperation
Reduced risk-taking during fasting is linked to more cooperative behaviour over shared resources, with participants demanding smaller shares from common pools.
2×
Cognitive effects
Food-related reminders impair reaction time and accuracy early in the fast, while inhibitory control measurably improves over the full month of practice.
4
Motive types
Fasters hold both autonomous (want-to) and controlled (have-to) motives. Want-to motives — intrinsic and identified — dominate and are more strongly linked to the fasting experience.
80%+
Prevalence
Over 80% of Muslims in majority-Muslim countries fast during Ramadan, making it one of the largest annual behavioural experiments in the world.
📡
Real-world measurement
The Jamasp platform enables large-scale wearable research via Fitbit and crowdsourcing, tracking heart rate, sleep, and activity in naturalistic settings.

The studies

Research programme

A sequence of experimental and longitudinal studies building from the cognitive lab to global field settings.

2022
PSPB

Temporary self-deprivation impairs cognitive control

A longitudinal randomised controlled study with N = 190 fasters. When reminded of food before an inhibitory control task, Ramadan participants made more errors and responded more slowly during than after Ramadan. Control participants — reminded of non-food stimuli — showed no such pattern, demonstrating the specificity of the effect.

RCT Cognition
2023
Motivation Science

Why do people fast? Motives, culture, and the fasting experience

A large-sample longitudinal study tracking fasting motives and experience before, during, and after Ramadan. Identified and intrinsic motives were consistently rated higher than external or introjected ones, and fasting culture in one's social environment predicted both motive type and perceived effort.

Longitudinal Motivation
2023
CRESP

From self-deprivation to cooperation: risk-aversion and the commons

Using a social resource dilemma and incentivised Balloon Analogue Risk Task, fasters showed significantly lower risk-taking during Ramadan than after. This shift mediated cooperative behaviour over shared resources, ruling out trust, social preferences, or reputational motives as alternative explanations.

Inhibitory control across the month of Ramadan

A study tracking changes in inhibitory control across the full 30-day fast, finding improvement over time consistent with the "self-regulatory muscle" account — practice in resisting impulses may strengthen control even as short-term costs appear early in each fasting day.

About the project

Why Ramadan? Why now?

Ramadan fasting is arguably the world's largest annual collective behavioural experiment. More than a billion people simultaneously alter their eating, sleeping, and daily routines for 30 days — and yet the psychological and social consequences of this transformation have barely been studied.

Psychology has long over-sampled Western, Educated, Industrial, Rich, Democratic (WEIRD) populations. Ramadan offers a window into a radically different form of goal-pursuit: collective, religiously motivated, socially enforced, and operating at civilisational scale.

This project, funded by the Templeton World Charity Foundation (grant #30295), set out to characterise the cognitive, motivational, and social effects of Ramadan fasting using rigorous experimental and longitudinal methods — and to develop open tools enabling future research in naturalistic settings worldwide.

Resources for researchers

Code, datasets, and infrastructure developed as part of this project, made available for the wider research community.

Jamasp

A prototype platform integrating Fitbit wearable data with the Prolific crowdsourcing panel. Enables recruitment, OAuth authorization, continuous data ingestion, and analysis pipelines for large-scale naturalistic behavioural research.

Experimental tasks
